Curatorial Statement

Orientalism is a category of art that refers to European depictions of Oriental (Eastern) subjects. Subjects often include areas including North Africa, the Middle East, and East Asia. This exhibit focuses on European depictions of the Middle East throughout history. While Orientalist works often were painted in a manner that appeared to document, they were very rarely neutral: often over-exoticising the Middle East, contributing to a reshaped Western perspective on "the East."

More recently, Orientalism has drawn criticism as being simplistic, stereotyped, and displaying inaccurate conceptions of Arab and Asian cultures ("Orientalism," Britannica). These works helped construct an 'imagined reality' for viewers: one catering towards European desire and politics. As scholar Edward Said argues, these distorted representations are a part of a broader system in which the west defines and controls how the Orient is perceived (Çelik 382).

The exotic nature of Oriental paintings manifests in hundreds of ways. For example, nudity is frequently used to suggest that it was a common occurrence in the historical Middle East, despite reality being on the contrary ("Orientalism," Smarthistory). This created a power structure which allowed 'othering' of middle eastern people on the basis of their geographic location through extreme depictions of their culture. Feminized, sexualized, and racialised paintings are common in Orientalist circles, which strongly distorted perception of consumers.

Orientalist artists were regularly displayed in public art exhibits, including at the Salon. The publicity forded to Orientalist artworks, thus, served a large part in shaping societal perspectives on the middle east. These mindsets were strongly in support of colonialism in the Middle East and North Africa ("Orientalism Art," Art in Context).

Although much of the artwork was historically inaccurate, studying Orientalism helps one understand the power dynamics that shaped colonialism. Furthermore, understanding Orientalism helps teach how historical viewpoints were altered, and allows us to contemplate how our mindsets are being shaped in the modern world through depictions given to us as reality. A thorough contemplation of Orientalist art reveals not only how historical biases were caused, but also an introspective feeling as to what biases shape our minds today.

As you explore the exhibit, keep in mind the power structure that shaped the images and granted them fame. Ask yourself questions like "what does this image make me try to feel," "what is being omitted," and "what is being purposefully included." Through doing that, you will realize how narratives were controlled surrounding the Middle East through art. By the end of the exhibit, you will have a glimpse into the colonialist mindset, and the narrative those in support of colonialism consumed.

The Collection

Works on Display

01

Work I of III

The Snake Charmer

Jean-Léon Gérôme

Date c. 1879 Medium Oil on Canvas Dimensions 32 3/8 × 47 5/8 in. Location The Clark Art Institute, Williamstown, Massachusetts

The Snake Charmer

Jean-Léon Gérôme, c. 1879

Jean-Léon Gérôme's painting The Snake Charmer is a sleazy imperialist vision of "the east" (Jones). The image depicts a nude boy with a snake in front of a crowd of men, adorned with Middle Eastern attire and arms. The background is a wall of Arabic calligraphy, with intricate tilework, reminiscent of Egyptian and Turkish architecture. This image, for many, is everything WRONG with orientalism. It depicts an exotic, fantasized version of the middle east. The artist, Jean-Léon Gérôme, likely never traveled to Egypt or Turkey, where this form of architecture was common ("Snake Charmer," Clark Art Institute). Thus, the image is imagined. But it seems real. It's that very illusion of authenticity which critics take issue with.

The painting is mysterious — it draws you in and makes you wonder as to the circumstances of the painting. In other words, it begs you to think about the world in which this event occurred — one as different and removed from the European world in which the viewers reside. In a large way, the emotions present in this painting become the emotions many viewers begin to have of the entire middle east.

As you ponder this painting, think: what mindsets does this intend to reinforce about the middle east?

02

Work II of III

The Turkish Bath (Le Bain turc)

Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres

Date 1852–59 Medium Oil on canvas glued to wood Dimensions 108 × 110 cm (42 1/2 × 43 5/16 in) Culture French Location Musée du Louvre, Paris

The Turkish Bath (Le Bain turc)

Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres, 1852–59

Tightly packed nude bodies. Instruments being played. Teacups littered in the immediate foreground. It screams orientalist.

The image exemplifies the erotic nature of Orientalist paintings. It's a depiction of a private bath made public — with characters conveniently painted and arranged for visual pleasure. The music playing seems to contribute to the relaxing, unwinding environment, in which women stretch and move in erotic manners. The painting seems as if it is giving viewers a glimpse into the reality of Turkish harems. It flattens the entire region to bathtime, and in no small part, an exoticised and carefully orchestrated bath time.

The women are of an extremely fair complexion, as well, which is uncommon for actual middle eastern audiences. Some darker skinned individuals are included, although they seem to be servants. Middle Eastern ceramics are on full display, with the intention of creating a mental association in the minds of viewers: Arab tilework = exotic Turkish bathhouses.

You, the viewer, are an unseen observer. The circular composition of the painting makes it seem as if you are peeping through a hole at the harem. You get to witness the sensual experience of the Turkish bath without being there — detached from presence, and privileged with pleasures.

As you observe, think: how does this painting inform colonialist viewpoints?

03

Work III of III

A Moorish Girl with Parakeet

Henriette Browne

Date 1875 Medium Oil on canvas Dimensions H 148 × W 92 cm Culture French Location Russell-Cotes Art Gallery and Museum

A Moorish Girl with Parakeet

Henriette Browne, 1875

A Moorish Girl with Parakeet. It's a beautiful depiction of… A Moorish Girl with a Parakeet.

The image features a young woman holding a parakeet in traditional attire. Henriette Browne was one of the few female Orientalist artists, and her work is vastly different from her male contemporaries. In her travels in the Middle East, she was granted access to female quarters, which allowed her to personally interact with the subjects of her paintings. As a result, her paintings are much less fantasizing (keep in mind, males never actually saw the harems which they painted). Thus, her paintings are much less objectifying.

This painting serves as a refreshing reminder that Orientalist paintings did not have to be exoticizing or feminizing or overtly sexualizing. Rather, they can depict day to day activities, and portray a more realistic, beautiful image of the Middle East.

Still, the work depicts the Moorish girl as different. She's more relatable than the women from the Turkish Bathhouse or The Snake Charmer, but still, slightly — different. Her clothes may be slightly performative, and the simplicity of a parakeet shifts the perspective to: "if it is so simple, it must be true to the daily life of everyone in the middle east."

Nonetheless, the painting A Moorish Girl with Parakeet is far different from the other Orientalist paintings on display in this exhibit.

As you ponder this painting, think: in what ways could other Orientalist artists have created a less exotic, more accurate depiction of the middle east?
